University of Notre Dame, United States.


I am a doctoral candidate at the University of Notre Dame (richter lab) and a National Center for Atmospheric Research (NCAR) advanced study graduate fellow. My research focuses on modeling air-sea interactions from submesoscale to turbulent scales and their impact on hurricane rapid intensification.

Specifically, I focus on understanding the dynamics of turbulence and organized structures (streaks, roll vortices, mesovortices and/or updraft-downdraft couplets) in the eyewall region of intense hurricanes and how they modulate hurricane structure and intensity changes.

Momentum Diffusion In Intense Hurricanes

The Role of Turbulence in Modulating Mean Wind Fields

This study uses a turbulence-resolving simulation of an idealized Category 5 tropical cyclone to understand the role of turbulence in these storms. Results show that turbulence clearly modulates the intensity and structure of these storms. Estimates of turbulent quantities (vertical eddy diffusivities and mixing lengths) agree reasonably well with previously observed values. In addition, we provide guidance for the values of these quantities in previously unobserved regions of intense storms.

This work (in collaboration with NCAR scientists) is currently under peer-review in the Journal of the Atmospheric Sciences.

Hurricanes and Climate change

Hurricane sensitivity to sea surface temperature

Tropical cyclones remain a major threat to the lives, property and economy of communities around the South West Indian ocean, notably Southern Africa and Madagascar. Tropical cyclones occur quite frequently around this region, hence the need to be adequately prepared for the impact of climate variability and climate change on them. Previous studies have shown that global warming due to anthropogenic forces may lead to an increase in the frequency of occurrence of tropical cyclones within the region. However, there is little work done regarding the impact of this on the intensity of tropical cyclones formed. This study uses the weather research forecast (WRF) model to perform a series of simulations for tropical cyclone Enawo with the aim of investigating the effect of an increase or decrease (by 2 degrees Celcius) in sea surface temperature (SST) on the intensity of the tropical cyclone (using windspeed, precipitation and pressure as measures of cyclone intensity). The experiment uses the data from European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) ERA5 re-analysis dataset to validate the results of the WRF model which was ran using boundary conditions data from climate forecast system reanalysis (CFSR).

The results indicate that the WRF model performs reasonably well in simulating the track and windspeed of the tropical cyclone, when compared to observational data. In simulating the tropical cyclone, the WRF model shows that an increase in the SST by 2 degrees Celcius generally increases the intensity of the tropical cyclone formed. This is evident in the increasing maximum precipitation rate as well as windspeed, and decreasing minimum pressure. An increase in SST also causes the emergence of a second low pressure system. On the other hand, a decrease in the SST by 2 degrees Celcius leads to a minute effect in the intensity but generally acts to decrease it. This results in a smoother track path for the tropical cyclone, a decrease in the maximum precipitation rate and windspeed, and an increase in the surface pressure.

The results of this study have shown that increasing the global temperature by around 2 degrees Celcius - violation of the Paris Accord - would lead to more violent and unpredictable tropical cyclones within the South West Indian ocean, and hence more destruction and loss of lives.

TROPICAL CYCLONE RAPID INTENSIFICATION (RI) ONSET TIMING

Impact of realistic multiscale SST anomalies on predictability

The characteristics of sea surface temperature (SST) anomalies in the tropical cyclone near-environment are inherently multiscale in nature as a result of interactions between various dynamical processes in the ocean. Assuming a uniform SST beneath storms in numerical simulations limits the predictability of how airâ€“sea interaction affects the physics of rapid intensification (RI).

In this study, the influence of realistic multiscale SST anomalies on RI onset timing is investigated. Our results suggest that the length scale of SST anomalies (in addition to its magnitude) modulate the distribution of convection, creating asymmetries around the RMW that can influence the predictability of RI onset. This effect is further modulated by storm translation speed, with the most prominent impact seen in slow-moving storms.

This work is currently published in the Journal of the Atmospheric Sciences.

Jacob Wenegrat

Assistant Professor
Department of Atmospheric and Oceanic Science
University of Maryland, College Park

I am a physical oceanographer, with a research focus on the ocean submesoscale (horizontal scales of 0.1-10 km). These small spatial scales are particularly challenging for traditional observational and computational methods, and understanding processes at these scales remains among the most pressing open challenges in oceanography and geophysical fluid dynamics. In my work I use a combination of theory and state-of-the-art high-resolution numerical simulations, with the goal being to gain deep insight into physical processes in the ocean and atmosphere. These insights are then extended to more complex physical settings through collaborations with observationalists and large-scale numerical modelers, with the goal of connecting fundamental ocean dynamics to broader interdisciplinary problems.

I did my undergraduate work at Stanford University, in an interdiscplinary program called Symbolic Systems, where I was primarily interested in what science could tell us about classic questions in mind-body philosophy. After college I worked for several years in technology consulting, and then spent 2 years living and traveling by sailboat. During this time I became interested in the field of oceanography, and became particularly excited by how theory, observations, and technology were being used together to answer fundamental questions about our earth system. I then did my PhD in Oceanography (with Mike McPhaden), and an MS in Applied Mathematics, at the University of Washington. For my postdoctoral work I returned to Stanford, where I worked with Leif Thomas on submesoscale ocean dynamics.
